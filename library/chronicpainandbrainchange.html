The Alarm That Won't Turn Off: Understanding Chronic Musculoskeletal Pain

Published on January 25, 2026 by Dr Desiree Lussier-Levesque

Imagine a smoke detector in a house that continues to blare long after the fire has been extinguished. At first, the sound was a vital warning that saved the structure, but as weeks turn into months, the alarm itself becomes the primary problem. It disrupts sleep, makes it impossible to concentrate, and eventually dictates how the residents move through every room in their own home. This is the reality of chronic pain—a condition where the body’s essential survival mechanism transitions from a protective tool into a persistent, life-altering burden that no longer serves a helpful purpose.

Chronic pain is not just a physical sensation; it is a complex experience that intertwines with emotional and psychological dimensions. It can lead to feelings of frustration, helplessness, and isolation. The constant presence of pain can overshadow daily activities, making even simple tasks feel daunting. Understanding chronic pain requires a shift in perspective—from viewing it solely as a symptom of an injury to recognizing it as a multifaceted condition that demands a comprehensive approach to management and care.

Addressing chronic pain effectively involves a combination of medical treatments, physical therapy, psychological support, and lifestyle modifications. Techniques such as mindfulness, cognitive-behavioral therapy, and physical rehabilitation can help individuals regain control over their lives. The goal is to not only alleviate pain but also improve overall quality of life, enabling individuals to engage in activities they enjoy and maintain meaningful relationships.

Acute versus Chronic

There is a critical biological distinction between the protection of acute pain and the burden of chronic pain. Acute pain is a survival reflex, like the sharp sting that makes you pull your hand away from a hot stove. However, chronic pain is defined as lasting or recurring for three months or longer, often continuing well after an injury has healed. At this stage, the pain is no longer adaptive; instead, well after an injury has healed. At this stage, the pain is no longer adaptive; instead, it begins to degrade overall health and the ability to function.

Chronic pain can arise from various conditions, including arthritis, fibromyalgia, nerve damage, and migraines. It can also persist without a clear underlying cause, making diagnosis and treatment more challenging. Unlike acute pain, which typically resolves with healing, chronic pain often requires ongoing management strategies to address its complex nature.

The Brain's Role

Chronic pain is not just a problem of the body; it is also a problem of the brain. The nervous system becomes sensitized, leading to an exaggerated response to pain signals. This phenomenon, known as central sensitization, means that even non-painful stimuli can be perceived as painful. The brain's plasticity allows it to adapt and change, but in the case of chronic pain, these changes can reinforce the pain experience, making it more difficult to treat.

Neuroimaging studies have shown that chronic pain is associated with measurable changes in the brain, such as those in regions involved in sensory integration and emotional regulation. These changes can affect mood, cognition, and behavior, further complicating the experience of chronic pain. This explains why the sensation of pain can feel impossible to ignore, even during quiet moments.

Exercise, Recovery, and Neuroplasticity

Chronic pain is a complex condition that requires a comprehensive approach to management and care. By understanding the interplay between physical, emotional, and psychological factors, individuals can better navigate their treatment journey and work towards improved quality of life. With the right support and strategies, it is possible to regain control and find relief from the persistent burden of chronic pain.

The brain possesses a remarkable quality called bidirectional plasticity, meaning that just as it can be molded by pain, it can also be remodeled toward recovery. This is where exercise plays a vital role as an intervention. Physical activity triggers the release of endogenous opioids and cannabinoids—the body’s natural chemistry for dampening pain signals. Movement also stimulates the production of the protein known as Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF), which supports neural health and the repair of circuits that may have been altered by long-term pain.

Engaging in consistent, manageable exercise can help retrain the nervous system, helping it to realize that activity is safe and potentially reducing sensitivity to pain triggers. While this process requires patience and a realistic approach to pacing to avoid overwhelming a sensitized system, research shows that as pain levels are managed and function returns, the structural changes in the brain can actually begin to normalize, demonstrating the brain's incredible capacity for resilience and change. The key is to start slowly and gradually increase intensity, allowing the body to adapt without exacerbating pain.

The Path Forward

The reality of chronic pain is that it changes how you navigate your life, but it doesn't have to be the end of the story. The nervous system is amazingly adaptable. Exercise is an incredibly effective tool for influencing that system. When you move in ways that feel safe and manageable, you are helping your body tap into its own natural chemistry to settle those loud pain signals. It’s not an overnight fix or a permanent cure, but a steady process of showing your brain that activity doesn’t have to equal danger. By working with your body where it is today, you are gradually expanding what you can do—aiming for a life defined by choices rather than limitations.
